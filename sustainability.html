    • When transporting goods and bulk commodity items over large distances, shipping by rail is an ideal option. For one, it is cheaper and more energy efficient than trucks. The transport industry is responsible for 1 billion tonnes of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions per year. Since GHG emissions are directly related to fuel consumption, expanding the use of rail is a good way to reduce greenhouse gas emissions without harming the economy.[Ibid] On average, railroads are 4 times more fuel efficient than trucks. That means moving freight by rail instead of truck reduces greenhouse gas emissions by 75 percent. According to CER (Community European Railway and Infrastructure Companies), if rail freight transport were doubled, with the freight shifted from the road sector, it could result in a reduction of GHG emission of around 45-55 million tonnes of CO2 a year. And if say, railways were to be the primary mode of freight transport by 2050, the estimated reduction would be 238 million tonnes of CO2 per year, equivalent to 19 percent of EU27 transport emissions in 2010.[1]

    EU27 share of CO2 emissions from fuel combustion by sector, 2011

    "Transport is responsible for around 20% of all GHG emissions within the EU28, and rises to 20% if emissions from international aviation and maritime transport are fully taken into account. It is the only sector where emissions have continued to rise since 1990. Rail accounts for just 0.6% of transport's GHG emissions through direct usage (diesel) and by around 1.5% if emissions from electricity generation are taken into account, even though railways represent 8.5% of transport activity." [2]

    Emission comparisons by mode

    "On average, rail is clearly more emissions-effficient and emits significantly lower CO2 emissions than comparable models of transport. A doubling of rail freight transport, with the freight shifted from the road sector, could result in a reduction of GHG emissions of around 45-55 million tonnes of CO2 a year. The vision for much greater use of rail by 2050 outlined in "Roadmap to a Single European Transport Area" would equate to around a 38% modal share rail freight transport and a 27% share for rail passenger transport. This would result in an estimated reduction of 238 million tonnes of CO2 a year, equivalent to 19% of EU27 transport emissions in 2010." [2]

    Freight emissions

    "The chart compares the total CO2 emissions emitted when transporting 100 tonnes of average goods from Basel, Switzerland to the port of Rotterdam, Netherlands. CO2 emissions from rail are eight times less than by road and almost seven times less than inland shipping." [2]

    Projected future railway CO2 emissions

    "From 1990 to 2010, the total CO2 emissions from the European railway sector were reduced by 39%. The strategy commits European railways to reduce their specific CO2 emissions by 50% by 2030, compared to 1990. In addition, by 2030 the European railways will cut their total CO2 emissions from train operation by 30% compared to the 1990 base year. Furthermore, by 2050 the European railways will strive towards complete carbon-free train operation, providing society with a climate-neutral transport option." [2]
